About Me

Researcher · Engineer · Builder

I am a PhD researcher at the University of Nebraska–Lincoln working at the intersection of wireless communications and intelligent systems. My research focuses on millimeter-wave (mmWave) communication, 5G/6G networks, and the application of machine learning to physical-layer wireless challenges.

I design and build experimental testbeds using software-defined radios (SDRs), develop wideband channel sounding systems, and investigate novel beam management strategies for next-generation wireless networks. My work bridges the gap between theoretical channel models and real-world propagation measurements.

I am targeting research engineer and RF engineering roles where I can apply my expertise in wireless system design, signal processing, and data-driven optimization to solve challenging problems in communications.

Research Highlights

Selected areas of ongoing and completed research

Vision-Based Beamforming

Leveraging computer vision and deep learning to predict optimal beam directions from visual scene information, enabling faster beam alignment and reducing overhead in mmWave communication systems.

Deep Learning mmWave Beam Prediction Computer Vision

mmWave Agricultural Channel Modeling

Conducting extensive propagation measurement campaigns in agricultural environments to characterize mmWave channel behavior, developing statistical models that capture the unique scattering and attenuation effects of crop canopies and terrain.

Channel Modeling Propagation Agriculture Measurement Campaigns

Wideband Channel Sounding Systems

Designing and deploying custom wideband channel sounders using software-defined radios for high-resolution characterization of wireless propagation channels, enabling accurate delay spread and angular analysis.

SDR Channel Sounding Wideband Testbed Design
